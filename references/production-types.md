# AI Realistic Human Drama: Two Production Types

## The Foundation

All content in this skill targets **AI Realistic Human Drama (AI仿真人剧)** —
photorealistic AI-generated visuals that simulate real actors, real cinematography,
and real production values. NOT comic/illustration/anime.

**Language Convention**: 剧本面向中国制作团队，产出面向美国观众。
- 对白/旁白/屏幕文字 = 英文
- 动作描述/镜头指示/制作注释 = 中文
- 人名/地名 = 英文（首次出现附中文译名）
- 详见 `references/bilingual-format-guide.md`

**Format Convention**: 正文交付使用中文短剧普通文本格式。旁白型/对白型只是叙事比例不同，不改变版式；仍写作 `角色：台词`、`旁白：...`、`角色VO：...`、`角色（OS）：...`。不要为旁白或对白另设框、表格、引用块或 screenplay 缩进块。

## Production Type A: Narration-Driven (旁白解说型)

### What It Is
Visual storytelling driven by a continuous voiceover narrator, supplemented by
character dialogue. The narrator serves as the audience's emotional guide,
providing context, internal thoughts, and plot bridges.

### Best For
- Emotional dramas with rich inner worlds
- Revenge/comeback stories where we need to hear the protagonist's calculations
- Stories with complex backstory that needs efficient delivery
- Female empowerment narratives where we need to hear her resolve

### Script Format (中英文混合格式示例)

```
第3集
3.1 曼哈顿董事会会议室 日 内
人物：Sarah、Mark

旁白：Five years. Two hundred sixty Mondays. And today, Mark finally made his last mistake.

△冷钢蓝色调，落地窗外是灰色的曼哈顿天际线。Sarah坐在会议桌远端，炭灰色西装外套，盘着权力髻，表情滴水不漏。
△MCU中近景，伦勃朗光勾勒出她半边脸的轮廓。

旁白：He thought he was promoting his niece. He didn't know he was handing me the keys.

△Mark把升职信推过桌面，得意洋洋。
Mark：Congratulations, Sarah. VP of Operations. You earned it.

△Sarah接过信，没有打开。她的笑意未达眼底。
Sarah：Thank you, Mark. I won't let you down.

Sarah（OS）：I wasn't thanking him. I was burying him.
```

### Narration Rules

| Rule | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Narrator voice** | Must have a distinct personality — not generic; can be protagonist, observer, or omniscient |
| **Show + tell balance** | Narrator provides what visuals CAN'T show (internal thoughts, backstory, emotional context) |
| **Never state the obvious** | If the visual shows anger, narrator says WHY she's angry, not THAT she's angry |
| **Rhythm** | Narration breathes between dialogue; never overlaps with spoken lines |
| **Emotional anchor** | Narrator's tone shifts with story arc — cold early, warm later, triumphant at karma moment |
| **Word economy** | Narration is the one place where slightly more words are allowed — but every sentence must EARN its place |

### Narration Dos and Don'ts

**DO:**
- "He said he loved me. The bank statements said otherwise." (contrast, irony)
- "Five rejection letters. One acceptance. I kept them all." (character depth)
- "I smiled. He believed it. That was his first mistake." (subtext, foreshadowing)

**DON'T:**
- "She was very angry." (states the obvious)
- "This was a difficult moment for her." (too generic)
- "She walked into the room. It was large." (describes what camera shows)

## Production Type B: Dialogue-Driven (对白演绎型)

### What It Is
Story told almost entirely through character dialogue and physical action,
with minimal or no narration. Like a traditional film scene — we understand
everything through what characters say and do.

### Best For
- Sharp, witty banter (enemies-to-lovers, workplace rivalry)
- High-tension confrontations
- Scenes with multiple characters debating/arguing
- Stories where subtext and reading-between-lines is the pleasure
- Romance where what's UNSAID is more important than what's said

### Script Format (中英文混合格式示例)

```
第3集
3.1 曼哈顿董事会会议室 日 内
人物：Sarah、Mark

△冷钢蓝色调，落地窗外灰色天际线。Sarah坐在会议桌远端，表情滴水不漏，双手将文件夹平铺在玻璃桌面上。
△Mark把升职信推过桌面，得意洋洋。

Mark：Congratulations, Sarah. VP of Operations. You earned it.
Sarah：Thank you, Mark. I won't let you down.
Mark（靠回椅背，心满意足）：I know you won't. That's why I picked you.

△Sarah终于打开信。扫视。表情没变，但眼睛周围微微收紧。
Sarah（平静，克制）：Operations. Not Strategy.

△Mark的笑容闪过一秒钟的裂缝。
Mark：Operations is where the company needs you.

△Sarah折起信，塞进西装口袋，站起来。
Sarah（直视他的眼睛）：Where the company needs me. Not where I earned. I see the difference.

△她走出去。Mark目送她离开，他的心满意足冷却成了某种更接近警惕的东西。
▲POV主观镜头：Sarah的背影消失在门外。
```

### Dialogue Rules

| Rule | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Subtext is everything** | Characters rarely say exactly what they mean; readers understand through implication |
| **Every line fights** | Even friendly lines have an edge; every conversation is a power negotiation |
| **Physical beats reveal truth** | When words lie, body language tells the truth — flat hands, jaw tightening, not blinking |
| **Silence is dialogue** | What a character DOESN'T say is as important as what they do |
| **American rhythm** | Contractions, fragments, interruptions — how real Americans talk under pressure |
| **The 30% cut** | After writing, cut 30% of words. If meaning survives, the cut version is better |

## Type Selection Guide

| Story Element | Narration-Driven (A) | Dialogue-Driven (B) |
|--------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Rich internal monologue | ✅ Perfect | ❌ Hard to convey |
| Complex backstory | ✅ Efficient delivery | ❌ Requires exposition |
| Sharp witty banter | ⚠️ Narrator can kill rhythm | ✅ Pure pleasure |
| Multi-character debate | ⚠️ Narrator adds distance | ✅ Immediate tension |
| Slow-burn romance | ✅ Narrator provides emotional temperature | ✅ Subtext is everything |
| Revenge/calculation | ✅ "I was burying him" | ✅ Physical tension |
| Action sequences | ✅ Narrator bridges cuts | ✅ Visual impact |
| Emotional reveals | ✅ `旁白：For the first time, I believed him.` | ✅ Character breaks silence |

### Hybrid Approach

Many hit series blend both types:
- **Setup episodes (1-5)**: More narration to establish context
- **Middle episodes (20-50)**: Shift toward dialogue as characters develop
- **Climax episodes (60-75)**: Minimal narration — let action speak
- **Resolution (76-80)**: Brief narration for emotional closure

The rule: **Let the story decide**. When the image + action can tell it, cut the narrator.
When we NEED to know what she's thinking, bring the narrator in.

## AI Production Implications

### For Narration-Driven
- Visuals can be more stylized (slow-mo, montage, atmospheric)
- Camera movement can be smoother (drift, glide)
- Transitions can be more fluid (dissolve, fade)
- Narrator audio must be recorded with cinematic quality

### For Dialogue-Driven
- Shot-reverse-shot editing essential for conversations
- Camera must capture facial expressions in detail (MCU/CU heavy)
- Lighting must support face-reading (no harsh shadows on faces during dialogue)
- Lip-sync must be precise if using AI talking head
- Silence requires strong environmental sound design
