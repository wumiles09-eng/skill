## 1. The Golden Triangle — Episode 1 Non-Negotiables

Source: Baiyang Studio (刘扬)

Within Episode 1, the viewer must clearly understand:

| Element | What It Means | Example |
|---------|--------------|---------|
| **Identity** | Who is the protagonist RIGHT NOW? | "She's the overlooked intern who actually runs the company" |
| **Crisis** | What immediate threat do they face? | "The CEO just announced layoffs — her department is first" |
| **Goal** | What do they actively want? | "She has 48 hours to prove her department is indispensable" |

**Why this matters**: The viewer decides whether to invest emotionally within the first 60 seconds. If they don't know who to root for, why to care, and what the stakes are, they scroll away.

**Bad example (vague)**: "Sarah is a woman with a complicated past who works at a company."
**Good example (specific)**: "Sarah (28, power-suit, corner office) slams her laptop shut. Her boss just took credit for her quarter-billion deal. She smiles — dangerously. 'Enjoy it, Mark. It's your last.'"

## 2. The Five Elements of a Golden Opening

Source: Baiyang Studio (刘扬)

### Element 1: Clear Character Establishment

The viewer must understand within seconds:
- What is their social status?
- What is their current emotional state?
- What is their defining trait?

**Technique**: Show through action, not exposition. Instead of VO saying "She's a struggling artist," show her painting over a rejected gallery notice.

### Element 2: Core Conflict Frontloaded

The central conflict must appear in Episode 1 — ideally in the first scene.

**Technique**: Open mid-crisis. The conflict is already underway when we meet the character. We learn who they are by watching them respond to pressure.

### Element 3: Emotional Burst Point

An emotional peak must occur within the first episode — shock, humiliation, betrayal, or triumph.

**Technique**: The emotional burst creates the bond between viewer and protagonist. If she's publicly humiliated, we feel it. If she's wronged, we want revenge on her behalf.

### Element 4: Strong Target/Direction

The protagonist must have a clear goal that drives the entire series.

**Technique**: The goal should be specific and measurable. "Get revenge" is weak. "Destroy his company before his IPO and take his corner office" is strong.

### Element 5: Effective Information Density

Every line of dialogue must carry multiple functions simultaneously.

**Functions per line**: Advance plot + reveal relationship + hint at subtext + establish voice

**Bad dialogue**:
```
SARAH
Hi, I'm Sarah. I've worked here for five years.
I'm really upset about what happened.
```

**Good dialogue**:
```
SARAH
Five years. Two hundred sixty Mondays. And you 
still don't know my name, do you, Mark?

                    (beat)
          Enjoy the view from my office. 
          It's temporary.
```

## 3. Dialogue as Bullets (Not Cotton)

Source: Yanbei Studio (燕北)

### The Core Principle

Short drama dialogue is **high-density compressed information**. Every line must be:
- **Direct**: No beating around the bush
- **Effective**: Every word earns its place
- **Loaded**: Multiple functions per line (plot + character + relationship + subtext)

### The 30% Cut Test

After writing dialogue, cut 30% of the words. If the core message survives, keep the shorter version. If meaning is lost, restore selectively.

### Dialogue Functions (in priority order)

1. **Drive plot** — Must move the story forward
2. **Reveal character** — Who is this person? What do they want?
3. **Show relationship** — Power dynamic, intimacy, hostility
4. **Control pacing** — Fast/slow rhythm matches scene energy
5. **Plant伏笔** — Setup for later payoff

### Show, Don't Tell (VO Rule)

- **Never use Voiceover (VO) when dialogue can do the job**
- **Never use on-screen explanatory text when action can show it**
- Transform internal thoughts into external actions or compressed dialogue

**Bad (VO tells us)**:
```
Sarah VO：I was furious. Five years of work, stolen.
```

**Good (action shows us)**:
```
△Sarah stares at the email. Her hand trembles.
△She crumples the paper, uncrumples it, photographs it with her phone.
Sarah（quiet, to herself）：Five years.
△She reaches for her coat. Not running — girding for war.
```

### The "Living Person" Test

Read dialogue aloud. If it sounds unnatural when spoken, rewrite it.

Signs of dead dialogue:
- Complete sentences where fragments would suffice
- Information the other character already knows
- Agreement phrases ("Yes, you're right") that don't advance anything
- Excessive politeness between enemies

### American-Specific Dialogue Notes

- Use contractions naturally: "I'm," "don't," "can't," "won't"
- Sentence fragments for tension: "You. Out. Now."
- Interrupt mid-sentence for conflict escalation
- Subtext over statement: "That's... generous of you" (meaning: I see your trap)
- Pop culture references: "He's giving main character energy," "She understood the assignment"

## 4. The Reverse-Engineering Method for Skill Building

Source: Yanbei Studio (燕北)

### Step 1: Watch a Hit Series

Select a series with 10亿+ views on Hongguo or top performance on ReelShort.

### Step 2: Transcribe Shot by Shot

Write the script equivalent of what you see on screen:
- Every action, every expression, every prop
- Every line of dialogue
- Every cut, every transition
- Timing of each scene

### Step 3: Analyze the Pattern

Identify:
- Where is the hook in each episode?
- Where is the cliffhanger?
- How many conflicts per episode?
- How is information revealed?
- What is left unsaid?

### Step 4: Write Your Own Version

Take the same structure, replace the content. If the hit series opens with a public humiliation at a gala, write a different public humiliation with your characters.

### Step 5: Compare and Iterate

Where does your version fall flat? Where is it better? Refine based on gaps.

**This is the single fastest way to develop short drama writing skill.**

## 5. IP Adaptation: Three Key Transitions from Novel to Script

Source: Yanbei Studio (燕北)

### Transition 1: From Free-Form Paragraphs to Standard Format

Novels use narrative prose. Scripts use:
```
1.1 地点 日 内
人物：角色A、角色B

△动作行（现在时、可拍、视觉化）。
角色A（语气/动作）：台词。
角色B：台词。
```

Every sentence must be filmable. "She felt sad" is not filmable. "She blinks rapidly, turns away, presses her palm to her mouth" is filmable.

### Transition 2: From Author Perspective to Audience Perspective

Novels can have internal monologue. Scripts must externalize everything.

**Technique**: Convert thoughts into:
- Physical reactions (hand trembling, jaw tightening)
- Verbal deflection (saying the opposite of what she feels)
- Action choices (what she does reveals what she thinks)

### Transition 3: From Long Setup to Immediate Conflict

Novels can spend chapters on worldbuilding. Scripts must conflict immediately.

**Rule**: The first scene should contain the inciting incident or its immediate aftermath. No peaceful morning routines. No slow establishing shots.

### The 50% Rewrite for Adaptation

When adapting Chinese IP for American audiences:
- Keep 50%: Emotional core, character relationships, core fantasy
- Rewrite 50%: Settings, cultural mechanisms, social logic, dialogue style

## 6. Micro-Innovation Strategies

Source: Baiyang Studio (刘扬)

### Strategy 1: Character Micro-Innovation

Take a familiar archetype and add one unexpected trait:

| Familiar Archetype | Micro-Innovation | Result |
|-------------------|-----------------|--------|
| CEO billionaire | He has severe social anxiety | Vulnerable power |
| Revenge heroine | She uses comedy as a weapon | Witty avenger |
| Sweet innocent | She's secretly a hacker | Hidden competence |
| Evil mother-in-law | She's actually protecting the heroine | Complex antagonist |

### Strategy 2: Setting Micro-Innovation

Move familiar dynamics to unexpected settings:

| Familiar Dynamic | New Setting | Fresh Tension |
|-----------------|-------------|---------------|
| Contract marriage | Family-owned vineyard | Legacy + romance |
| Office rivalry | Startup incubator | Survival stakes |
| Secret identity | Witness protection program | Life-or-death stakes |
| Enemies to lovers | Rival food trucks | Competition + chemistry |

### Strategy 3: Expression Style Innovation

Same trope, new delivery:

- **Comedy-drama hybrid**: "The Office" meets contract marriage
- **Mockumentary style**: Characters break fourth wall
- **Reverse chronology**: Start at the end, show how we got there
- **Dual timeline**: Past/present parallel revealing connections

### The Core Rule: "Old wine in new bottles"

The emotional beats that work are ancient. What differentiates your series is the **packaging** — fresh setting, unexpected character detail, innovative delivery style.

## 7. Common Pitfalls for New Writers

Source: Baiyang Studio + Yanbei Studio

### Pitfall 1: Slow Opening ("Pacing" mistake)

**Problem**: Spending 2-3 episodes establishing normal life before conflict arrives.
**Fix**: Start the conflict in Scene 1. Show normal life in 10 seconds of contrast, then disrupt it.

### Pitfall 2: Redundant Dialogue ("Cotton" mistake)

**Problem**: Characters chat about nothing for half the episode.
**Fix**: Apply the Bullet Principle. Every line must earn its place. Cut 30% and test.

### Pitfall 3: Vague Character Goals ("Why should I care?" mistake)

**Problem**: Protagonist is passive, reacting to events rather than driving them.
**Fix**: Give her an active goal in Episode 1. She wants something specific and takes action to get it.

### Pitfall 4: Over-Explaining ("Distrust the audience" mistake)

**Problem**: VO narration, OS text, characters stating what the audience can see.
**Fix**: Trust visual storytelling. Show through action and reaction. Cut all VO that isn't essential.

### Pitfall 5: Stale Execution ("Copy without understanding" mistake)

**Problem**: Mimicking surface elements of hit series without understanding why they work.
**Fix**: Reverse-engineer hit series (see Section 4). Understand the emotional function behind each beat.

### Pitfall 6: Outdated Tropes ("Behind the market" mistake)

**Problem**: Using tropes that were popular 6 months ago but are now oversaturated.
**Fix**: Daily market monitoring. Check ReelShort/DramaBox trending lists. What's climbing? What's declining?

### Pitfall 7: Weak Episode 1 ("Save the good stuff" mistake)

**Problem**: Holding back the best content for later episodes.
**Fix**: Your Episode 1 IS your sales pitch. If it doesn't hook, there is no Episode 2. Lead with your strongest material.

## 8. The Hit Series Definition (Industry Standard)

Source: DataEye + TikTok for Business

A "hit series" (爆款) meets ALL of these criteria:

| Metric | Threshold | What It Means |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **3-day spend** | ≥$20,000 | Ad spend in first 3 days of promotion |
| **CTR (Click-Through Rate)** | ≥1.5% | Percentage of viewers who click to watch |
| **6s completion rate** | ≥20% | Percentage who watch past the first 6 seconds |
| **Revenue concentration** | Top 2% generate ~80% of revenue | Hit series carry the market |

### The Hit Series Lifecycle

- **Development**: 2-4 weeks (script + pre-production)
- **Production**: 7-15 days (shooting)
- **Post-production**: 1-2 weeks (editing, music, grading)
- **Promotion**: 35-40 days active lifecycle
- **Revenue peak**: Days 3-10 of promotion

### The 2% Rule

Only ~2% of released series become true hits. The difference between a hit and a miss:
1. **Script quality** (40%): Story, characters, hooks, pacing
2. **Production value** (20%): Acting, cinematography, editing
3. **Promotion strategy** (25%): Ad creatives, targeting, budget
4. **Timing/Luck** (15%): Market conditions, competition, trends

**As a writer, you control the 40%. Make it count.**
